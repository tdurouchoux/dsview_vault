## Summary

The article by Toby Ord discusses the concept of a "half-life" for the success rates of AI agents based on recent findings by Kwa et al. (2025). The key points include:

- AI agents show an exponentially declining success rate on longer tasks, implying a constant rate of failure that can be modeled mathematically.
- The performance of AI agents on various tasks reveals a predictable relationship: every 7 months, the length of tasks they can successfully complete doubles.
- Success rates for AI agents drop as task duration increases; the 50% success rate serves as a critical threshold for modeling performance.
- There exists a notable difference between achieving 50% and higher success rates (e.g., 80%); for instance, the best agents have much shorter time horizons for higher thresholds.
- The paper introduces the constant hazard rate model, suggesting that the probability of success or failure is stable over time, leading to exponential decay in performance on longer tasks.
- The findings raise questions about the differential performance of human versus AI, suggesting the need for deeper investigation into the underlying mechanisms of success and failure.
- Predictive rules are established for future AI capabilities based on this model, including expectations for time horizons at various success rates.

Overall, while the results provide important insights into the scaling of AI performance, there are concerns regarding the generalizability of these findings across different tasks. Future work is needed to validate these models and assumptions.
