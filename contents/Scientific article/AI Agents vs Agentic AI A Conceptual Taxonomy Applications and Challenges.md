## Summary

This review distinguishes between AI Agents and Agentic AI, establishing a conceptual taxonomy, mapping applications, and analyzing challenges to highlight their differing design philosophies and capabilities. AI Agents, characterized as modular systems that utilize large language models (LLMs) and large image models (LIMs), are focused on narrow, task-specific automation and predominantly employ generative AI. They enhance functionality through tool integration, prompting engineering, and reasoning capabilities.

In contrast, Agentic AI signifies a significant shift toward multi-agent collaboration with features such as dynamic task decomposition, persistent memory, and complex autonomy. The paper discusses the evolution of these systems, contrasting operational mechanisms, interaction styles, and levels of autonomy across paradigms.

Application domains for AI Agents include customer support, scheduling, and data summarization, whereas Agentic AI finds uses in research automation, robotic coordination, and medical decision-making. The review further contemplates the unique challenges faced by both paradigms, such as hallucinations, brittleness, and coordination failures, proposing potential solutions like feedback loops and causal modeling.

The authors suggest a roadmap for building scalable and explainable AI systems. The methodology adopted for this review incorporates a multi-stage approach to assess the evolution, application, and limitations of both AI Agents and Agentic AI, laying a foundation for future developments in the field.
