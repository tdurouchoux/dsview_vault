## Summary

This paper investigates the performance and limitations of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) compared to standard Large Language Models (LLMs) by evaluating their reasoning capabilities in controllable puzzle environments of varying complexity. The study identifies three key regime categories based on problem difficulty: 

1. Low-complexity tasks where LLMs outperform LRMs.
2. Medium-complexity tasks where LRMs demonstrate advantages through deeper reasoning.
3. High-complexity tasks where both types collapse in performance.

The findings reveal that LRMs struggle significantly beyond certain complexity thresholds, exhibiting a counterintuitive drop in reasoning effort as task difficulty increases, despite having the capacity for higher token budgets. 

The analysis includes extensive experiments on puzzles such as the Tower of Hanoi, Checkers Jumping, River Crossing, and Blocks World, highlighting that while LRMs can generate detailed reasoning processes, they often fail in consistent logical computation, unable to utilize explicit algorithms effectively. 

Overall, LRMs show strengths in moderate problem-solving but face inefficiencies and scaling limits in high-complexity scenarios, raising critical questions about the nature of their reasoning capabilities and highlighting the need for a reevaluation of current evaluation paradigms that rely heavily on final answer accuracy alone. The results prompt further investigations into optimizing reasoning processes and improving LRM design for more robust generalization in reasoning tasks.
