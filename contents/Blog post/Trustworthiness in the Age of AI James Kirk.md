## Summary

The content discusses the evolving nature of trust in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly regarding algorithms and large language models (LLMs). It highlights the historical reliability of computers as calculators, with confidence in deterministic outputs. However, with the advent of Big Data and probabilistic algorithms, the perception shifted towards viewing algorithms as fallible, where trust is contingent upon the creators rather than the technology itself.

As AI, particularly LLMs, emerges, there is a growing tendency to trust these systems due to their seemingly human-like capabilities. Yet, LLMs function differently from traditional computers; they do not hold concrete knowledge, leading to potential inaccuracies. The nuances of how users perceive and trust AI contrast with the straightforward trust associated with traditional calculators and algorithms.

Key points include:
1. The transition from computers as infallible calculators to algorithms that reflect creator trustworthiness.
2. The disparity between how LLMs are perceived (as trustworthy agents) versus their unpredictable nature (as fallible tools).
3. The challenge faced by engineers in instilling trust in AI, considering the systems demand trust while failing to consistently deliver reliability.
4. The need for a new model of trust that acknowledges AI's potential for deception and the accountability of its creators.

Ultimately, the author emphasizes that while AI can solve complex problems, it remains essential for data scientists and engineers to critically evaluate their creations and understand the implications of their reliability and use in practice.
