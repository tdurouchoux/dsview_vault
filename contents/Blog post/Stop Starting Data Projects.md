## Summary

The article outlines a practical process for managing data projects effectively, emphasizing stakeholder engagement and iterative delivery. Key points include:

1. **Initial Response**: Avoid diving into technical solutions immediately. Instead, respond to vague requests with a simple message to open a conversation without escalating to a formal project prematurely.

2. **Stakeholder Engagement**: During the first call, ask stakeholders to walk through their current process and define a clear "definition of done" with a single agreed-upon sentence. Identify key stakeholders (decision-makers, champions, and informed parties) early to avoid misalignment.

3. **RACI Framework**: Informally define roles (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) to clarify responsibilities and accountability, ensuring quick decision-making when issues arise.

4. **MVP in 5 Days**: Build a minimal viable product (MVP) within five working days to validate direction early. Use hardcoded values and placeholder logic to focus on solving the right problem, not perfection.

5. **Continuous Feedback**: Share progress every three days to maintain stakeholder engagement and prevent projects from going silent. Address feedback promptly to avoid misalignment.

6. **Handling Misalignment**: If stakeholders express dissatisfaction, ask for clarification and adjust the MVP accordingly. Sometimes, the project may be unnecessary, saving time and effort.

7. **Closing Projects**: Deliver when the project is "good enough," not perfect. Close the project with a final check for critical missing elements and document the outcome.

8. **Scope Management**: Avoid scope creep by deferring additional requests to future iterations. Clearly define when a project is complete to prevent over-engineering.

The process emphasizes communication, early validation, and stakeholder involvement to ensure technical work is adopted and valued. The author argues that success in data projects hinges on non-technical rigor as much as technical skill.
