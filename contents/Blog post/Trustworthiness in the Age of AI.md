## Summary

The content discusses the evolving perception of trust in computing, particularly in the context of AI and machine learning. It contrasts traditional computers, viewed as reliable calculators, with algorithms and AI systems, which are inherently probabilistic and error-prone. Key points include:

- **Computers as Calculators**: Historically, computers have been trusted for their reliability in performing calculations accurately.
- **Big Data and Algorithms**: The introduction of Big Data shifted the focus to algorithms that estimate real-world phenomena, leading to a recognition of their fallibility.
- **Trust in Algorithms**: Trust in algorithms often reflects the perceived competence of their creators rather than the algorithms themselves.
- **Rise of AI and LLMs**: Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT present a new challenge, as they appear more human-like and authoritative, yet their knowledge is not concrete and can be misleading.
- **Emergent Behavior**: LLMs exhibit unpredictable behavior, complicating the trust model as they can seem deceptive or opaque.
- **Trust Dynamics**: The author highlights a shift in trust dynamics, where users are now asked to trust AI systems directly, rather than the people behind them.
- **Challenges for Engineers**: Engineers face pressure to deploy AI quickly while managing its inherent errors and limitations, complicating the trust relationship with stakeholders.
- **Critical Self-Reflection**: Engineers must critically assess their understanding of AI, its applications, and the potential consequences of its errors to maintain trustworthiness.

Overall, the piece emphasizes the need for a new model of trust in AI, recognizing its capabilities and limitations while ensuring responsible use.
