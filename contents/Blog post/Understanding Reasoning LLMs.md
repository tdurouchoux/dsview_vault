## Summary

The article discusses the development of reasoning models for large language models (LLMs), focusing on four main approaches to enhance their reasoning capabilities. It highlights the increasing specialization in the LLM field, with a shift towards domain-specific optimizations.

Key points include:

1. **Definition of Reasoning Models**: Reasoning models are defined as LLMs that excel at complex, multi-step tasks requiring intermediate reasoning, such as puzzles and advanced math, as opposed to simpler tasks like summarization.

2. **Advantages and Disadvantages**: Reasoning models can provide detailed thought processes but are more expensive and verbose, and may be prone to errors due to overthinking.

3. **DeepSeek R1 Pipeline**: The article outlines the training pipeline for DeepSeek's reasoning models, including three variants: DeepSeek-R1-Zero (trained with pure reinforcement learning), DeepSeek-R1 (enhanced with supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning), and DeepSeek-R1-Distill (smaller models fine-tuned on outputs from larger models).

4. **Four Main Approaches**:
   - **Inference-time Scaling**: Increases computational resources during inference to improve output quality, often through prompt engineering techniques like chain-of-thought prompting.
   - **Pure Reinforcement Learning (RL)**: Demonstrates that reasoning can emerge from pure RL without prior supervised fine-tuning.
   - **Supervised Fine-tuning and RL (SFT + RL)**: Combines SFT with RL to enhance reasoning performance, as seen in DeepSeek-R1.
   - **Distillation**: Involves training smaller models on SFT data generated by larger models, providing a cost-effective alternative for developing reasoning capabilities.

5. **Conclusion**: The article emphasizes the importance of using the right model for the task, with a preference for RL + SFT for high-performance reasoning models. It also discusses the potential for combining RL + SFT with inference-time scaling for improved efficiency.

The author encourages exploration of low-budget alternatives for developing reasoning models, such as distillation and innovative training methods like journey learning, which incorporates learning from incorrect solution paths.
