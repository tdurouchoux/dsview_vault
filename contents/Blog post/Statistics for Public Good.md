## Summary

The article discusses the need for official statistics in the UK to better serve the public rather than primarily focusing on government needs. Paul Allin highlights the Royal Statistical Society's (RSS) campaign advocating for "public statistics," which emphasizes a question-based approach to identify societal needs for data. While official statistics like CPI and GDP are crucial for government decision-making, there is a tendency to prioritize government users over the general public. The RSS argues for a broader role of statistics to empower citizens and enhance public engagement with important societal issues.

The article references the United Nations' principles for official statistics, which stress the importance of serving the public good. It critiques the UK government's response to the Lievesley review, which suggested a consultative process for statistical priorities but noted that government needs often take precedence. The RSS aims to leverage the increasing availability of data beyond official statistics to address public interest questions and foster a more engaged citizenry. The vision includes a collaborative effort to improve data quality and accessibility, ultimately enhancing decision-making and societal outcomes.
