Implicit biases in machine learning refer to inherent tendencies in models or optimization processes that favor learning simpler, more generalizable functions without explicit design or regularization. These biases can arise from architectural choices (e.g., neural network structures), optimization algorithms (e.g., gradient-based methods), or the nature of the data itself. For example, neural networks may inherently favor simpler functions due to the way their parameters are initialized or optimized. Implicit biases help explain why models often generalize well despite having the capacity to memorize training data, and they play a crucial role in bridging the gap between theoretical generalization bounds and empirical observations in deep learning.
