Hallucination in language models, particularly in large language models (LLMs), refers to the phenomenon where the model generates outputs that are factually incorrect, nonsensical, or completely fabricated, yet presented with high confidence. This issue is critical in contexts such as coding, scientific research, and natural language processing, where accuracy is paramount. Hallucinations can manifest as incorrect code implementations, fabricated APIs, misattributed information, or overconfident falsehoods. 

The causes of hallucinations are multifaceted, including factors such as the model's training data, task complexity, insufficient context, and evaluation procedures that may reward guessing over acknowledging uncertainty. While newer models have reduced the frequency of hallucinations, they remain a significant challenge, necessitating rigorous validation and testing of AI-generated outputs. 

Various strategies, such as fine-tuning, selective generation, and benchmarking tools like FACTS Grounding, are employed to mitigate hallucinations and improve the reliability and trustworthiness of language model outputs.
