Architectural biases refer to the tendencies of neural network architectures to favor learning certain types of functions over others, independent of the training data or optimization process. For example, deeper networks or specific architectural choices (e.g., skip connections) may bias models toward simpler, more generalizable solutions. Research has shown that even randomly initialized neural networks are more likely to implement simpler functions, which can contribute to better generalization. These biases arise from the way architectures constrain the hypothesis space of possible solutions, making some functions easier to learn than others.
